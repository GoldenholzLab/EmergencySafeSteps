The 4 Emergency Messages

These are the core safety messages used across the SafeSteps tools. The goal: clear, simple actions anyone can follow during a seizure emergency.

1

Nothing in the mouth

Do not put anything into the person’s mouth. The myth that someone will “swallow their tongue” is false. Objects in the mouth can cause injuries or choking.

2

5 minutes? Call emergency services

If the convulsive seizure lasts longer than about 5 minutes, it is a medical emergency. Call your local emergency number (e.g., 911 in the United States).

3

Protect the head

Move hard or sharp objects away, and use pillows, clothing, or other soft items to protect the head and body from injury.

4

Lay on the side

When it is safe, gently roll the person onto their side to help keep the airway clear and reduce the risk of aspiration if they vomit.

SafeSteps tools express these messages without using words like “epilepsy” or “seizure” to reduce stigma.

Why SafeSteps?

Seizure emergencies rarely happen in controlled environments. Handouts and formal teaching are important, but they may not be available at the moment an emergency occurs.

The SafeSteps idea: bring a small, durable, physical object directly into that moment – something that lives on a keychain, in a wallet, or on a wall, and that reminds caregivers what to do.

Design goals

  • Always nearby: keychain, pillbox, card, or sign
  • Heat-stable, durable, recyclable (e.g., PETG)
  • Clear, concise, stigma-free language
  • Usable worldwide, across many languages

Who is behind this?

The SafeSteps Platform is led by Daniel Goldenholz, MD, PhD, FAES, Director of the Epilepsy + Data Science Lab at Harvard Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center. He is a board-certified epileptologist and is very motivated to improve life for people with epilepsy.

Project focus

  • Seizure emergency education for caregivers and bystanders
  • Open-source, improvable 3D designs
  • Global accessibility via multiple languages and formats

For Makers and 3D Printing Enthusiasts

SafeSteps is intentionally designed to be shared, remixed, and improved by the worldwide 3D printing community.

What you need

  • FDM 3D printer (multicolor required for some of our designs)
  • Heat-stable, recyclable materials such as PETG
  • Basic familiarity with multi-color printing workflows

Single color 3D printers can be purchased for less than US$200. Many consumer multicolor 3D printers under ~US$500 can handle all these designs; more advanced printers can provide extra benefits.

Design workflow

Early prototypes were made in TinkerCAD. Over time, designs moved into more advanced tools such as Fusion 360, VR design with Gravity Sketch, and AI-assisted parameterized designs in OpenSCAD for flexible customization.

The OpenSCAD approach allows rapid generation of variants (e.g., different emergency numbers, languages, and formats), while keeping the core logic consistent.

SafeSteps Tools

Multiple form factors share the same core safety messages, so different people can choose what fits their life best. All designs are open source (free to download and print without restriction).

SafeSteps T (Tactile Keychain)

A two-color 3D-printed keychain, about the size of a standard house key, with slightly raised letters for tactile support. It is designed to withstand being scratched by keys while staying readable.

Because it includes the local emergency number, there are separate versions for different countries. Sets are available for English-speaking countries with the appropriate emergency codes.

Download model files on Printables

SafeSteps NFC

A clean, text-free fob designed for people who prefer not to have visible wording. When a modern smartphone is held near the NFC tag, it opens a simple website with the same SafeSteps emergency instructions.

Download NFC model files

SafeSteps QR

A 3D-printed QR code keychain. Scanning it with a smartphone camera brings up a simple website explaining seizure safety steps. Unlike a paper QR code, the 3D-printed version is built to be durable and always on hand.

Download QR model files

SafeSteps Sign

A simple sign approximately the size of a business card, suitable for hanging, magnet mounting, or carrying in a wallet. Generated using the same AI-assisted system as SafeSteps T, it supports multiple languages and emergency numbers.

Download sign model files

SafeSteps Pillbox

A pillbox with compartments for each day of the week and a sliding cover. The four emergency messages are printed on the sides, and versions are available for multiple languages and country codes.
